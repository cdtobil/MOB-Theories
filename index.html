Organisational Behaviour

Management Theories
Applied

Scenario-based questions identifying motivational and management theories in real Ghanaian workplace contexts.

10 Questions · 10 Theories
01
BOST Manufacturing

Employees who meet their monthly targets are publicly praised during meetings and given performance bonuses. Workers who consistently underperform are excluded from team incentives and may be reassigned to less desirable roles.

Theory Identified
Reinforcement Theory

Employees are rewarded (bonuses, praise) for good performance and punished (exclusion, reassignment) for poor performance — a classic application of positive and negative reinforcement.

02
Adom Tech

Management believes employees dislike work and must be closely supervised. Supervisors monitor staff strictly, enforce rules rigidly, and rarely delegate decision-making authority.

Theory Identified
Theory X

Management assumes employees are inherently lazy and dislike work, and therefore require strict supervision, control, and little autonomy — the defining characteristics of McGregor's Theory X.

03
GoldFields Ghana

Employees are motivated because they believe that putting in extra effort will lead to high performance, which will then result in promotions and salary increases.

Theory Identified
Expectancy Theory

Employees are motivated because they believe effort leads to performance, and performance leads to valued rewards — the core Effort → Performance → Reward chain of Vroom's Expectancy Theory.

04
Zenith Bank

Management ensures employees have good salaries, job security, and safe working conditions. However, they notice that even after improving these, employees are still not highly motivated to go above and beyond.

Theory Identified
Two-Factor Theory

Hygiene factors (salary, safety, job security) prevent dissatisfaction but do not create strong motivation on their own. True motivation requires motivators such as recognition, responsibility, and growth — the essence of Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory.

05
Kasapreko Company Ltd

Workers feel dissatisfied because they believe their pay is lower than colleagues in similar roles at competing firms, despite performing at the same level.

Theory Identified
Equity Theory

Employees compare their inputs and outcomes with those of others. When they perceive their pay as unfairly lower for equivalent performance, dissatisfaction results — the central premise of Adams' Equity Theory.

06
MTN Ghana

Employees are grouped into teams where collaboration and social interaction are encouraged. Management notices that workers perform better when they feel a strong sense of belonging within their teams.

Theory Identified
Human Relations Theory

The theory emphasises social needs, teamwork, and a sense of belonging as key motivators. Performance improves when employees feel connected and valued within their groups — rooted in Mayo's Hawthorne studies.

07
Accra Startup

Management allows employees to set their own goals, take initiative, and develop creative solutions. Workers feel fulfilled because they are growing and reaching their full potential.

Theory Identified
Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Employees are motivated by self-actualisation — the highest level of Maslow's pyramid — which involves growth, creativity, autonomy, and reaching one's full potential.

08
Retail Company

Employees receive a fixed salary regardless of performance. Over time, productivity drops because workers feel there is no link between effort and reward.

Theory Identified
Expectancy Theory

When there is no perceived link between effort and reward, the expectancy chain breaks down and motivation collapses — a failure scenario that directly illustrates Vroom's Expectancy Theory.

09
Coca-Cola Bottling Company

A manager changes her leadership style depending on the situation — being strict when deadlines are tight and more supportive when employees are learning new tasks.

Theory Identified
Contingency Theory

There is no single best leadership style. The manager adapts her approach based on the demands of each situation — the core principle of Contingency (Situational) Leadership Theory.

10
Logistics Firm

Employees are motivated primarily by financial rewards. Management introduces commissions and bonuses, leading to a significant increase in performance.

Theory Identified
Reinforcement Theory

Financial rewards (commissions, bonuses) act as positive reinforcers that strengthen desired employee behaviour, producing measurable improvements in performance — a direct application of Skinner's Reinforcement Theory.
